Our Why

A retirement project, not a developer pitch.

I am looking at 527 Scott Street as a preservation project first. Not as a flip, not as a luxury conversion, and not as a way to erase a church and market the shell. The reason the building matters is the sanctuary. That is the part that gives it weight, memory, and purpose.

For me, this is also a retirement project and a family legacy project. The idea is to bring my family into a building that needs daily care, practical repair, and a real use. Jeremy and his family, Noah, and I would each have a stake in keeping the place alive. The family use is not meant to replace the church identity. It is meant to make stewardship possible.

The long-term vision is a building where the sanctuary remains the heart: music, readings, worship, community meetings, small arts gatherings, and other uses that fit the space instead of fighting it. Around that, the working parts of the building could support stained glass, lamp making, welding, theater work, design, and youth arts programming.

There are real questions. Zoning, fire code, residential use, insurance, parking, accessibility, historic review, financing, and construction all need professional answers. This site is not claiming those answers are solved. It is a way to explain the intention clearly: preserve the sanctuary, listen before acting, and build a credible path only if Baltimore officials, neighbors, preservation people, and practical experts can help us do it correctly.

The Building

Brick, sanctuary, parsonage, workrooms, and unanswered questions.

The building should tell us what it can become. The plan should not force it into something generic.

History to verify

The site is associated with a historic Scott Street church presence in Pigtown. The next step is confirming the church history through records, photographs, title history, neighborhood memory, and Baltimore preservation sources.

Condition to document

Before-and-after documentation should start with the existing sanctuary, balcony, stained glass, brick, roof, structure, utilities, water intrusion, life-safety conditions, and any prior alterations.

Photos to add

Use honest photographs: brick, doors, sanctuary volume, stained glass, workshop possibilities, damaged areas, and the surrounding block. No fake renderings that imply approvals or finished work.

Sanctuary

The main room stays the main room.

The sanctuary is not leftover square footage. It is the center of the project. The working assumption is that the room stays recognizable as a sanctuary and remains available for uses that fit its character: music, reflection, worship, readings, neighborhood meetings, and arts gatherings.

Any future seating, lighting, sound, accessibility, fire-safety, or code work should support the room’s original dignity. The goal is not to make it slick. The goal is to make it usable, safe, warm, and alive.

Family Workshops

Living there means caring for it every day.

Family residential concept

The attached parsonage and related spaces may be explored for three family households or caretaker-style use, but only if Baltimore confirms what is lawful and feasible in writing.

Craft and repair

Stained glass, lamp making, theater work, design, and small-scale repair belong naturally in a building shaped by handwork.

Noah’s making space

Welding and fabrication concepts would need proper code, ventilation, fire protection, insurance, and separation. The idea is creative work, not unsafe improvisation.

FAQ

The real questions need written answers.

Can Baltimore allow residential, arts, and community use in a church building?

That has to be confirmed by Baltimore officials and qualified local counsel. The working idea is church preservation with possible parsonage, caretaker, family, arts, and community uses supporting the building. No final use is being represented until zoning, occupancy, code, and legal review are confirmed in writing.

What about fire code and life safety?

This is a major issue. Assembly space, residential areas, workshops, exits, alarms, sprinklers, accessibility, and occupant load all need professional review. The project should assume life-safety work may drive cost and feasibility.

What about parking and neighborhood impact?

The project should avoid making promises about events or public programming until parking, loading, noise, hours, occupancy, and neighbor concerns are understood. The intention is small, appropriate use, not event-hall marketing.

Will historic review or CHAP matter?

Possibly. The team needs to verify whether any local, state, or federal historic review applies and whether historic tax credits, preservation easements, or design guidance could help or restrict the project.

Is financing solved?

No. Financing, tax credits, grants, construction lending, insurance, and acquisition structure all need review. The site is meant to open serious conversations, not pretend the deal is complete.

Is this a church, a home, an arts center, or a community space?

The best short answer is: a preservation and adaptive-use vision centered on keeping the sanctuary alive. The exact legal and operational structure has to follow what Baltimore will allow and what the building can safely support.
